\begin{abstract}
Physics-Informed Extreme Learning Machines (PIELMs) offer a one-shot alternative to iterative Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for solving partial differential equations. However, their accuracy is highly sensitive to the weight scale hyperparameter $R_m$, which must be tuned per problem---often requiring expensive search procedures. We combine sinusoidal activation~(following recent Fourier-based PIELM work) with log-uniform weight initialization, a power-law distribution ($\alpha \approx 1$) that allocates neurons uniformly across frequency decades---an idea explored by Tancik et al.\ for deep Fourier feature networks but not yet studied in the PIELM setting. Through systematic experiments on five linear PDE benchmarks---and a generality study on four additional problem types with non-sinusoidal structure---we demonstrate three results: (i)~at tight neuron budgets ($h \leq 40$), log-uniform initialization outperforms Gaussian and uniform by up to $300\times$ in $L_2$ error, because every basis function covers a distinct frequency scale; (ii)~an ablation over the power-law exponent $\alpha$ reveals that $\alpha \approx 0.75$--$1.25$ is optimal, while heavier tails ($\alpha = 2$) degrade accuracy by up to seven orders of magnitude; (iii)~a scale sweep shows that log-uniform degrades more gracefully than Gaussian when $R_m$ is over-specified, reducing the need for per-problem tuning. All training uses the PDE residual and boundary conditions only---no reference solution is required.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ===================================================================

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)~\cite{raissi2019physics} embed partial differential equation (PDE) residuals into the training loss, enabling mesh-free solvers that learn solutions from the governing equations. Despite their flexibility, PINNs require thousands of gradient-descent iterations and suffer from spectral bias---a systematic preference for low-frequency solution components that slows convergence on multi-scale problems~\cite{rahaman2019spectral,wang2022pinns}.

Extreme Learning Machines (ELMs)~\cite{huang2006extreme} bypass iterative training entirely: the hidden-layer weights are frozen at random, and only the output weights $\beta$ are solved analytically via least-squares. Dwivedi and Srinivasan~\cite{dwivedi2020pielm} extended this idea to PDEs as Physics-Informed ELMs (PIELMs), applying the differential operator to the random basis analytically, and solving a single linear system that enforces the PDE residual and boundary conditions without any reference solution.

A central limitation of PIELMs is their sensitivity to the weight scale $R_m$. Dong~\cite{dong2022computing} showed that accuracy can vary by orders of magnitude as $R_m$ changes, and proposed differential evolution to find the optimal value---an expensive search that partially negates the one-shot advantage of ELMs. This scale sensitivity arises because all standard initialization schemes (Gaussian, uniform) concentrate weights in a narrow band of effective frequencies, so the basis can only resolve solution features near that band.

In this work, we address the scale sensitivity problem through two complementary ideas:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sinusoidal activation for PIELM.} Following recent work on Fourier-based PIELM~\cite{li2023fpielm,ren2025gffpielm,sitzmann2020siren}, we use $\phi(x) = \sin(x)$ instead of $\tanh$ or Gaussian RBF. For $\sin(w_j x + b_j)$, the PDE operator yields exact, closed-form expressions:
    \[
        \frac{d^2}{dx^2}\sin(w_j x + b_j) = -w_j^2 \sin(w_j x + b_j),
    \]
    so the PIELM system matrix can be assembled without finite differences or automatic differentiation. Crucially, each neuron becomes an explicit Fourier mode with frequency $|w_j|$, making the weight initialization directly interpretable as a spectral allocation problem.

    \item \textbf{Log-uniform weight initialization.} We draw the weight magnitudes $|w|$ from a log-uniform distribution on $[1, R_m]$, which allocates equal probability to each decade of frequency. Tancik et al.~\cite{tancik2020fourier} explored log-uniform sampling for deep Fourier feature networks and concluded that only the standard deviation of the frequency distribution matters, not its shape. We show that this conclusion does \emph{not} transfer to the PIELM setting: when the neuron budget is tight, distribution shape becomes critical because there is no iterative training to compensate for a poor basis.
\end{enumerate}

Our experiments yield three main findings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low neuron budgets.} When the hidden dimension $h$ is small ($h \leq 40$), log-uniform initialization outperforms Gaussian by $10$--$300\times$ in $L_2$ error, because every neuron covers a distinct frequency scale rather than duplicating others (Section~\ref{sec:neuron_budget}).
    \item \textbf{Optimal exponent.} An ablation over the power-law exponent $\alpha$ in $P(w) \propto |w|^{-\alpha}$ shows that $\alpha \approx 0.75$--$1.25$ (near log-uniform) is optimal; $\alpha = 2.0$ degrades accuracy by up to seven orders of magnitude on multi-frequency problems (Section~\ref{sec:alpha_ablation}).
    \item \textbf{Scale robustness.} A sweep over $R_m$ shows that log-uniform degrades more gracefully than Gaussian when the scale is over-specified, reducing the need for per-problem tuning (Section~\ref{sec:scale_sweep}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Physics-Informed ELM with Sinusoidal Basis}
\label{sec:pielm}

Given a linear PDE $\mathcal{L}[u](x) = f(x)$ on domain $\Omega$ with Dirichlet boundary conditions $u|_{\partial\Omega} = g$, PIELM approximates the solution as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:trial}
    \hat{u}(x) = \sum_{j=1}^{h} \beta_j \, \sin(w_j x + b_j),
\end{equation}
where the weights $\{w_j, b_j\}$ are frozen at initialization and only $\beta \in \mathbb{R}^h$ is solved for.

\paragraph{Exact PDE operators.}
Because $\sin(\cdot)$ is an eigenfunction of differentiation, the PDE operator applied to each basis function has a closed form. For the Poisson equation $-u'' = f$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:poisson_op}
    -\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\sin(w_j x + b_j) = w_j^2 \, \sin(w_j x + b_j).
\end{equation}
For the Helmholtz equation $-u'' - k^2 u = f$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:helmholtz_op}
    \bigl(-\tfrac{d^2}{dx^2} - k^2\bigr)\sin(w_j x + b_j) = (w_j^2 - k^2)\, \sin(w_j x + b_j).
\end{equation}
For advection--diffusion $-\varepsilon u'' + u' = f$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:advdiff_op}
    \bigl(-\varepsilon \tfrac{d^2}{dx^2} + \tfrac{d}{dx}\bigr)\sin(w_j x + b_j) = \varepsilon w_j^2 \sin(w_j x + b_j) + w_j \cos(w_j x + b_j).
\end{equation}
No finite differences or computational graphs are needed---the feature matrix is assembled analytically.

\paragraph{Linear system.}
Let $\{x_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ be interior collocation points and $\{x_k^{\text{bc}}\}$ boundary points. Define the PDE feature matrix $A_{\text{pde}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times h}$ with entries $[\mathcal{L}[\phi_j]](x_i)$, and boundary feature matrix $A_{\text{bc}}$ with entries $\phi_j(x_k^{\text{bc}})$. The PIELM system is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:system}
    \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} A_{\text{pde}} \\ \lambda_{\text{bc}}\, A_{\text{bc}} \end{bmatrix}}_{A}
    \beta =
    \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} f_{\text{int}} \\ \lambda_{\text{bc}}\, g_{\text{bc}} \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{r}},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{\text{bc}}$ is a penalty weight for boundary enforcement. The output weights are obtained via Tikhonov-regularized least-squares:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:solve}
    \beta = (A^\top A + \lambda I)^{-1} A^\top \mathbf{r}.
\end{equation}
The entire training procedure---from weight generation to $\beta$---is a single forward pass with cost $O(N h^2 + h^3)$.


\subsection{Log-Uniform Initialization}
\label{sec:loguniform}

Standard ELM initializations draw weights from distributions concentrated at a single scale:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gaussian}: $w_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_m)$ --- weights cluster near zero; most neurons produce similar low-frequency features.
    \item \textbf{Uniform}: $w_j \sim \mathcal{U}(-R_m, R_m)$ --- weights are uniformly spaced in magnitude, over-representing high frequencies near $R_m$.
\end{itemize}

We propose \textbf{log-uniform initialization}: the magnitude $|w_j|$ is drawn uniformly in log-space over $[0, \log_{10} R_m]$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loguniform}
    |w_j| = 10^{u_j}, \quad u_j \sim \mathcal{U}(0,\, \log_{10} R_m),
\end{equation}
with an independent random sign $\text{sgn}(w_j) \in \{-1, +1\}$ and bias $b_j \sim \mathcal{U}(-\pi, \pi)$.

This places equal probability mass on each decade of frequency (e.g., $[1, 10]$ and $[10, 100]$ each receive $50\%$ of neurons when $R_m = 100$). In the language of power-law distributions~\cite{clauset2009powerlaw}, log-uniform corresponds to $P(|w|) \propto |w|^{-\alpha}$ with $\alpha = 1$, the boundary between light-tailed ($\alpha < 1$) and heavy-tailed ($\alpha > 1$) regimes.

\paragraph{Connection to random Fourier features.}
For sinusoidal activation, $\sin(w_j x + b_j)$ is a random Fourier feature~\cite{rahimi2007random,tancik2020fourier}. The weight distribution controls the \emph{spectral measure} of the kernel approximated by the ELM. Gaussian weights yield a Gaussian kernel (narrowband), while log-uniform weights yield a broadband kernel whose spectrum decays as $1/\omega$---a natural match for physical systems with energy distributed across scales.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%% ===================================================================

All experiments use the PIELM formulation (Eq.~\ref{eq:system}--\ref{eq:solve}) with $\lambda_{\text{bc}} = 100$, $\lambda = 10^{-10}$, $N = 200$ interior points, and double precision arithmetic. The reference solution $u_{\text{true}}$ is used \emph{only for evaluation} (computing $L_2$ relative error); it is never seen during training.

\subsection{Benchmark Problems}

We evaluate on five linear PDEs with known analytical solutions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Poisson 1D:} $-u'' = \pi^2 \sin(\pi x)$ on $[-1,1]$, $u(\pm 1) = 0$. Solution: $u = \sin(\pi x)$.

    \item \textbf{Multi-frequency Poisson:} $-u'' = f(x)$ on $[-1,1]$ with manufactured solution $u = \sin(\pi x) + 0.3\sin(3\pi x) + 0.1\sin(7\pi x)$, combining three spatial frequencies.

    \item \textbf{Helmholtz ($k=10$):} $-u'' - 100\,u = f$ on $[-1,1]$ with $u = \sin(\pi x)\,e^{-4x^2}$, a localized wave packet requiring both oscillatory and envelope features.

    \item \textbf{High-frequency Poisson:} $-u'' = (10\pi)^2 \sin(10\pi x)$ on $[-1,1]$, testing the ability to resolve a single high-frequency mode ($\omega = 10\pi \approx 31.4$).

    \item \textbf{Advection--diffusion:} $-\varepsilon u'' + u' = 0$ on $[0,1]$, $u(0)=0$, $u(1)=1$, $\varepsilon = 0.02$. The solution exhibits a sharp boundary layer near $x = 1$.
\end{enumerate}

To test generality beyond these five core benchmarks, we also evaluate on four additional problem types with non-sinusoidal solution structure (Section~\ref{sec:generality}, Appendix~\ref{app:diverse}):

\begin{enumerate}\setcounter{enumi}{5}
    \item \textbf{Helmholtz ($k=20$):} Same form as benchmark 3 but with $k=20$, requiring higher-frequency basis functions.

    \item \textbf{Polynomial $\times$ oscillation:} $-u'' = f$ on $[-1,1]$ with $u = x(1-x^2)\sin(5\pi x)$, combining polynomial envelope and oscillation.

    \item \textbf{Gaussian $+$ oscillation:} $-u'' = f$ on $[-1,1]$ with $u = e^{-10x^2}\sin(\pi x) + 0.3\sin(7\pi x)$, combining a localized Gaussian pulse with a high-frequency global mode.

    \item \textbf{Product trigonometric:} $-u'' = f$ on $[-1,1]$ with $u = \sin(\pi x)\cos(3\pi x) = \tfrac{1}{2}[\sin(4\pi x) - \sin(2\pi x)]$, a product of two frequencies that decomposes into two modes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Initialization Strategies}

Three weight initializations are compared:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Log-Uniform (proposed):} $|w_j| = 10^{u_j}$, $u_j \sim \mathcal{U}(0, \log_{10} R_m)$, $\text{sgn}(w_j) \sim \{-1,+1\}$, $b_j \sim \mathcal{U}(-\pi, \pi)$.
    \item \textbf{Gaussian:} $w_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_m)$, $b_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_m)$.
    \item \textbf{Uniform:} $w_j \sim \mathcal{U}(-R_m, R_m)$, $b_j \sim \mathcal{U}(-R_m, R_m)$.
\end{itemize}
Unless otherwise stated, we use $R_m = 30$, $h = 400$ hidden neurons, and 20 independent random seeds per configuration.

At $\alpha \leq 1$, the distribution is flat or increasing in log-space, ensuring adequate coverage of all frequency bands.


\subsection{Scale Sweep}
\label{sec:scale_sweep}

To address the question raised by Dong~\cite{dong2022computing}---whether the initialization benefit is merely a scale effect---we sweep $R_m \in \{1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50, 80, 100, 150\}$ independently for each initialization.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{exp2_scale_sweep.png}
    \caption{$L_2$ error versus weight scale $R_m$ for four benchmarks. Shaded regions: $\pm 1$ std over 10 seeds. Gaussian (blue) exhibits the narrowest optimal band and degrades by $10^4$--$10^5$ at large $R_m$. Log-uniform (red) remains within $10^2$ of its optimum across the full range tested.}
    \label{fig:scale_sweep}
\end{figure}

At each initialization's \emph{optimal} $R_m$, the errors are comparable (all within one order of magnitude). This confirms Dong's finding that scale is the primary determinant of accuracy when the scale is well-tuned.

However, the degradation curves differ qualitatively. For multi-frequency Poisson at $R_m = 100$: Gaussian error $= 5.4 \times 10^{-4}$, log-uniform error $= 7.7 \times 10^{-8}$---a $7{,}000\times$ gap. This robustness to over-specification is the practical advantage: in deployment, one cannot always tune $R_m$ per problem, and log-uniform provides a safe default.

Two regimes emerge. On Helmholtz ($k{=}20$) and the product-trigonometric problem, log-uniform achieves $L_2 < 1\%$ while Gaussian and uniform fail entirely ($L_2 > 0.2$)---the same pattern as the core benchmarks. On the remaining three problems, $h = 20$ is genuinely insufficient for any initialization: even log-uniform exceeds $6\%$ error. However, log-uniform still degrades more gracefully, maintaining $2$--$15\times$ lower error than Gaussian. Figure~\ref{fig:diverse} in Appendix~\ref{app:diverse} shows solution fields and pointwise errors for six representative problems (including Helmholtz $k{=}20$ at a second, tighter budget $h{=}15$), visualizing both the ``log-uniform solves'' and ``log-uniform degrades gracefully'' regimes.

These results confirm that the low-budget advantage is not specific to the five core benchmarks: it extends to any problem whose solution contains multi-scale frequency content, regardless of whether the solution is a pure sinusoidal sum.


%% ===================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{When Does Initialization Matter?}

Our results delineate two regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Over-parameterized ($h \gg$ required):} All initializations achieve comparable accuracy ($\sim 10^{-9}$). The basis is rich enough that frequency coverage is guaranteed regardless of distribution shape. In this regime, Dong's~\cite{dong2022computing} finding that scale matters more than distribution holds, consistent with Tancik et al.'s~\cite{tancik2020fourier} observation that only the standard deviation matters for deep networks with iterative training.
    \item \textbf{Budget-constrained ($h \leq 60$):} Initialization becomes critical. Log-uniform's frequency diversity produces a well-conditioned system with few neurons, while Gaussian wastes capacity on redundant features. This is the regime where Tancik et al.'s conclusion breaks down: without iterative training to compensate, the PIELM must capture the full solution in a single linear solve, and distribution shape determines whether the basis spans the required frequency range. This regime is the relevant one for real-time and edge deployment.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Effect of Heavier Tails ($\alpha \geq 2$)}

One might expect heavier-tailed distributions ($\alpha \geq 2$) to improve spectral coverage by producing more extreme weights. Our ablation shows the opposite for PDE solving. At $\alpha = 2$, the density $P(|w|) \propto |w|^{-2}$ assigns most probability to the smallest weights, producing a basis dominated by low-frequency functions. For problems with multi-scale structure (which is the common case in physics), the optimal exponent is $\alpha \approx 0.75$--$1.25$, corresponding to a near-uniform allocation in log-space.

\subsection{Sinusoidal Activation for PIELM}

The sinusoidal activation $\sin(w_j x + b_j)$ has recently been adopted in PIELM variants for biharmonic equations~\cite{li2023fpielm} and high-frequency PDEs~\cite{ren2025gffpielm}. Our work complements these studies by using the sinusoidal basis to expose how weight \emph{initialization}---rather than activation choice---controls solution quality. Because each neuron is explicitly a Fourier mode with frequency $|w_j|$, the weight distribution directly determines the spectral coverage of the basis. This interpretability enabled the frequency-based analysis in Sections~\ref{sec:neuron_budget}--\ref{sec:spectrum}, connecting initialization shape to approximation-theoretic properties of the random feature space.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Nonlinear PDEs.} PIELM requires the PDE operator to be linear in $\beta$. Nonlinear equations (e.g., Burgers) require iterative linearization or time-stepping, losing the one-shot advantage.
    \item \textbf{Single high-frequency targets.} When the solution contains a single high-frequency mode (e.g., $\sin(10\pi x)$), Gaussian initialization can be superior because it concentrates neurons near the target frequency. Log-uniform spreads its budget across all decades and under-allocates the critical band.
    \item \textbf{Boundary layers.} The advection--diffusion benchmark reveals that global sinusoidal bases struggle with sharp, localized features. Localized activations (Gaussian RBF) or domain decomposition~\cite{dong2021extreme} may be preferable in such cases.
    \item \textbf{Higher dimensions.} All experiments are 1D. Extension to 2D and 3D introduces the curse of dimensionality for collocation-based methods and requires further study.
\end{enumerate}


%% ===================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%% ===================================================================

We introduced two modifications to the Physics-Informed ELM framework: sinusoidal activation for exact PDE operator assembly, and log-uniform weight initialization for multi-scale frequency coverage. The main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item At low neuron budgets ($h \leq 40$), log-uniform initialization outperforms Gaussian by up to $300\times$ on Helmholtz and $15\times$ on multi-frequency Poisson, by ensuring every neuron covers a distinct frequency scale.
    \item The power-law exponent $\alpha \approx 0.75$--$1.25$ is optimal; heavier tails ($\alpha = 2.0$) degrade accuracy by up to seven orders of magnitude on multi-scale problems, because they concentrate neurons at low frequencies rather than spreading them across scales.
    \item Log-uniform initialization degrades more gracefully than Gaussian when the weight scale $R_m$ is over-specified, reducing the need for the expensive scale-tuning procedures proposed by Dong~\cite{dong2022computing}.
\end{enumerate}

A generality study on four additional problem types---Helmholtz at higher wavenumber, polynomial-modulated oscillations, Gaussian-localized modes, and trigonometric products---confirms that these findings extend beyond pure sinusoidal solutions to any PDE with multi-scale frequency content.

These results position log-uniform PIELM as a practical choice for resource-constrained settings where both speed and robustness matter---edge computing, real-time surrogate models, and rapid prototyping of PDE solutions. Future work will extend the framework to multi-dimensional problems, nonlinear PDEs via iterative PIELM, and adaptive allocation of neurons to frequency bands informed by the PDE structure.
